Shot List & Cinematography FAQ

Questions that might come up about working on shot lists — and what I've learned running productions.

1. How do I create a shot list for a movie?
A shot list is your scene-by-scene plan — all the ideas and decisions that transform the essence of the script, and the way your director wants to tell the story, into concrete shots. For each scene, you break it down into individual shots: size (close-up, medium, wide), angle, camera movement, lens choice, and the specific passage of the script each shot covers. But as a cinematographer that's not everything to consider. You read the scene, make notes, discuss ideas with your director, identify important moments — what the scene is really about, which perspective you want to be in, what the atmosphere, the lighting mood, the colors should be, what's emotionally important. As a cinematographer you think a lot about lens choices, choreography, color, decisions in other departments like production design or costume. All of that feeds into lighting, and that can involve a lot of planning and preparation. There's a lot of communication with those departments, and more and more information and ideas accumulate for each scene. It's crucial to organize them well and forward information to your colleagues. Others use spreadsheets. Dedicated apps like CINEside, Shot Lister, or StudioBinder give you a screenplay on one side, your shots on the other, and let you anchor each shot directly to the script. That's the best solution in prep, when things are changing a lot and new script versions come in — in those apps you can edit everything on the go at your desk. Personally, I still like to have a printed-out script once principal photography starts. It doesn't need a battery, it doesn't break if it falls off the dolly seat, and I can quickly write a note in it. Paper doesn't love water either, but if a script page gets sand on it or a bit wet on a beach shoot, you can still read it — a laptop usually can't say the same. And there's something about an actual paper script, lined with pencil marks, corners folded over, creased from being in your pocket all day. It starts to feel like the work itself. Others prefer iPads or other devices, and that's fine — everyone works differently.

2. What's the difference between a shot list and a shooting script?
They're related but not the same. A shot list is a flat list of every shot for each scene of the movie — each with its technical and creative details: size, angle, lens, camera movement, the script passage it covers, plus notes for the departments involved. It's a collaborative document, built in conversation with the director and shared across the whole crew. A shooting script is the full screenplay marked up with scene numbers, shot breakdowns, department notes, and often visual references placed inline next to the passages they belong to. Same information, different structure — organized around the script itself rather than around the shots. Most cinematographers work with both, depending on what's needed in the moment. The shot list is easier to scan for planning the day. The shooting script is closer to the story, which helps when discussing intent with actors, the director, or any department working scene by scene. Besides the scheduling moments when I'm just putting the shots in order, it's important to me to have the script with all its narrative and emotional content in sight while thinking about shots. That's how the images stay connected to what the scene is actually about. Modern tools like CINEside let you build both simultaneously — your shots anchored to the script, exported as a single facing-pages PDF.

3. Is there a good offline shot list app for Mac?
Yes — though fewer than you'd expect. Most modern shot-list tools are cloud-based: StudioBinder, Stencil, ShotKraft, and most web apps. They sync your work across devices and let teams collaborate from anywhere. The tradeoff is that your screenplay lives on their servers — and that you need a stable internet connection to work. For a lot of productions, that's fine. For others, it's not an option at all. Sometimes it's the legal side — franchises, studio projects under NDA, unreleased material where the producer explicitly rules out cloud tools. More than once I've been on projects where that came up on day one. Other times it's simply where you're shooting. I've worked in the woods of Texas on The Honor Farm, on a beach at the Baltic Sea shooting Marlene, remote farmhouses, old buildings with no coverage. Plenty of places where connectivity is bad or non-existent. You want to pull up your shot list during a scout or on the morning of a shoot, and the app just doesn't load. Even when you do have signal, a laptop or iPad on a sandy beach, in the rain, or in snow is its own problem — electronics and the elements don't mix. The offline options on Mac are narrower: CINEside — built specifically around this. Your script, shots, references, and notes all stay on your machine. Nothing syncs anywhere unless you choose to export. Works the same way at your desk, on a mountain, or on a plane. Shot Designer — Mac and iPad, offline-capable, more focused on camera diagrams and blocking than on full shot-list prep. Spreadsheets and paper — still a valid choice, especially for smaller productions. Just harder to keep in sync across revisions. If you work mostly on material where cloud is fine and you always have a connection, the convenience of web tools wins. If you regularly handle NDA-protected scripts, or find yourself on locations where connectivity is unreliable — or where the environment is rough on electronics in the first place — offline stops being a preference and becomes a requirement.

4. Can I import a Final Draft screenplay into a shot list app?
Yes. Most modern tools accept PDF exports from screenwriting software — Final Draft, WriterDuet, Fade In, Highland, Celtx, or anything else. You export your screenplay as PDF from your writing tool, then import that PDF into the shot-list app. The quality of the import varies a lot between tools. A good one does more than just display the PDF: It detects scene headings automatically (INT./EXT. ... — DAY/NIGHT) and creates a structured scene list It keeps the script text searchable and selectable, not a flat image It handles revised drafts — when a new version comes in, it can match it against your existing project and keep your shots, notes, and references attached to the scenes that still exist That last part matters more than it sounds. Screenplays change during prep, sometimes heavily. On Tales of Franz, the script kept evolving right up to the start of principal photography — and in a good way. A lot of the changes came directly out of the shot-list work with the director. When you sit down and picture what you actually want to see on screen for a scene, you start thinking differently about the whole film. Questions come up that aren't really about the shot list — they're about the story, the character, the structure. Those conversations feed back into the script. At the time I didn't have the right tool yet. I assembled photos and text fields across different apps and stitched them into a PDF. Every new script version meant adapting the whole system by hand. When the AD needed the shot list in a different format, I did the reorganizing manually. Some days I felt more like an office administrator than a cinematographer. That's one of the reasons I started building CINEside. The problem gets sharper when you're working remotely. More and more of my early prep happens with a director in the US while I'm in Europe — sharing script ideas, references, photos, drawings, notes across time zones and tools. On There There, which we shot on iPhones during Covid, that remote process went as far as I've ever taken it. If CINEside had existed then, There There is the project I would have wanted it for. CINEside handles this with a two-mode import — full replacement for major rewrites, or a revision merge that keeps your work attached where scenes still match. A review step lets you confirm anything that's ambiguous. The goal is simple: you should be able to welcome a new draft instead of dreading it.

5. Is my screenplay safe when I use a shot list app?
Depends entirely on the app. Cloud-based tools upload your screenplay to their servers. This is convenient for team collaboration, but means your script lives on a third party's infrastructure. Read their terms — especially around data retention, AI training rights, and who can access your content. Offline tools like CINEside or Shot Designer store everything locally. Your screenplay never leaves your computer unless you explicitly send it. But "send it" matters. The moment you email a project file to your director, it's no longer strictly offline — it's on your mail server, their mail server, and whatever services handle the transit in between. If that's a concern for a particular project, there are safer ways to hand over a file: a USB stick passed directly, an encrypted drive, or a secure file-transfer service your producer has approved. Offline tools don't force you into the cloud, but they also don't solve the question of how you move files. That part stays with you. For pre-release productions, franchise work, or anything under strict NDA, offline is the professional standard. Many studios and showrunners require it.

6. How do I share my shot list with the director?
Three common approaches: PDF export. Export your shot list or shooting script as PDF and email it. Simple, universal, works with anyone. Downside: no way for them to comment back in-context. Cloud collaboration. Tools like StudioBinder let you invite the director to your project online. They can view and comment, but they need an account and a subscription slot. Dedicated companion app. CINEside uses a different approach: you export a package file (.cnct), your director opens it in CINEside CONNECT — a free companion app — adds their notes to shots, script passages, and images, and sends their notes back as a separate file. Everything stays offline on both sides. The right approach depends on how your director prefers to work and how sensitive the material is.

7. Do I need a subscription for shot list software?
No — it depends on the tool. Subscription-based tools charge monthly or yearly. StudioBinder runs from around $42 to over $300 per month depending on the plan. Shot Lister has paid tiers. Most web-based tools are subscriptions by default. The model makes sense if you're a production company with parallel shoots and teams that need to collaborate, or if you want every feature update as it ships. The tradeoff is continuity — your access to the tool, and often to your files inside it, depends on keeping the subscription active. I lived through a version of that with Photoshop. I used CC for years, then at some point decided I didn't want to keep paying the monthly fee — and suddenly I couldn't open my own files anymore. That stays with you. Since then, I've gotten more careful about which tools I want to be paying rent on, and which I'd rather own outright. One-time purchase tools let you pay once and keep the software. CINEside is €79 in early access — a one-time purchase, with free updates and no subscription. Shot Designer is around €20. Older desktop tools sometimes fall in this category too. This model makes sense for individual cinematographers who prep a project, wrap it, and may not touch the tool for months between jobs. Your projects stay openable as long as the app still runs on your Mac. Free and freemium tiers exist on several platforms — StudioBinder has a free tier limited to one project, Shot Designer has a free single-scene mode. These work well for trying things out, for short-form work, or for commercials and music videos where the scope fits within the limits. For longer, more complex productions, the paid tiers usually become necessary. The right answer depends on how you work. A working cinematographer who preps a handful of projects a year often gets more value from a one-time purchase — the cost is small and the project files stay openable years later. A production company with parallel shoots and teams that need to collaborate may find the recurring features of a subscription worth the cost. Different workflows, different tools.
